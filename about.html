Purpose and Context

Our project Sound of Swearing is the first of its kind in relating sound symbology to linguistics and phonetics. We set out to determine what connection there was between words considered vulgar and how they sounded. We were particularly curious about how the results compared across languages, and how that correlation related to the word's etymology, as can be seen on the graphic on the home page. This project is partially based on the concept of ideophones, which are specific words used to convey sensory images. The sounds of these words usually relate to the sensory image they are describing. One study conducted by Wolfgang Kohler in 1929, and repeated in 2001, asked a group of English speakers and a group of Tamil speakers to both pick a name for two shapes, shown below.

There was a

98% preference

to name the jagged shape ‘Kiki’ and the rounded shape ‘Bouba’. This study was the first of its kind in the correlation of words across languages that developed relatively isolated from each other. This was the piggyback for our study.

The field of linguistics already has some scholarship relating languages together and other scholarship discussing the reasoning for certain sounds, however none that specifically looks at context-driven sound similarity across languages, as we set out to do. Some of the key knowledge of the field as it relates is detailed below.


Evelyn Altenberg’s 2005 journal article details similarities in the way different languages are spoken. Specifically in this study, Altenberg looks at how people distinguish word breaks in a second language in which they have no knowledge of.


Jack Fellman’s 1973 journal article explores the socio-cultural similarities between languages part of the same family, which added context to our decision to compare languages by family. Through his research, it reasons to suggest that the context of words (say vulgarities for example) may be related at a deeper level of the language tree.


William Austin’s 1957 journal article describes criteria for evaluating phonetic similarity across languages. His work was crucial to the creation of the phonetic groups and mannerisms we use today.


Wolf Leslau published two journal articles, one in one in 1952 and one in 1959, both looking at Ethiopian specifically. In them, he details much of why certain words are taboo or vulgar in a language and how they may sound similar to non-taboo words in other languages.

Mannerisms

Nasal - Consonants where sound escapes through the nose (‘m’, ‘n’, ‘-ng’)
Stop - Consonants where airway is blocked completely by the tongue (‘t’, ‘d’) or by the body (‘g’, k’) or lips (‘b’,’p’)
Sibilant Fricative - Consonants where the tongue directs air at a sharp angle into the teeth, which are held close together (‘s’, ‘z’, ‘ch’)
Non-Sibilant Fricative - Consonants where air flows between a narrow channel in the mouth (‘f’, ‘v’, ‘th’)
Approximant - Consonants where speech articulators approach each other, but don’t create much turbulence in the air, similar to vowels (‘w’, English ‘r’, ‘y’)
Flap-Tap - Similar to a Stop consonant, a flap is a single contact between two articulators, however, without a buildup and release (Spanish ‘r’, or the ‘tt’ in ‘latter’)
Trill - Consonant produced by vibration (rolled ‘r’)
Lateral Fricative - Consonant where tongue blocks most of the airway, but air escapes to the sides of the tongue, laterally. Does not exist in English.
Lateral Approximant - Similar to an Approximant, but the air escapes around the side of the tongue (‘L’)
Lateral Flap-Tap - Again, similar to a flap-tap, except the air escapes to the sides of the tongue. No examples in English.
Ejectives - Consonants with a distinct buildup before release, different that stops. Does not exist in English.
Clicks - Clicking sounds, generally found in East and South African languages
Implosive - Group of stop consonants where the air flows towards lungs, and can be modified by pitch. Does not exist in English.

NOTE: the included examples are not the only sounds in the manner, more were excluded for concision.



Research Team Bio

We are a team of college students seeking to understand the relationship between the meaning and sound of words. The project was first created as a final project in the honors class "Digital Humanities" at The University of Texas at Dallas.
