Conclusions

With the data and graphs provided, it’s quite clear to see there is a distinct difference in every language in at least one manner. However, it is quite hard to generalize what is seen across every single language, however, listed below is observations we see.


In 77% of all cases where the NASAL manner featured a significant change, the frequency in vulgarities DECREASES as compared to the language average, by an average of -5.98%.

In 66% of all cases where the STOP manner featured a significant change, the frequency in vulgarities INCREASED as compared to the language average, by an average of +6.35%.

43% of SIBILANT FRICATIVES did not show significant change.

81% of the languages studied showed significant variation in the NON-SIBILANT FRICATIVE manner. Those that showed a significant decrease on average decreased by -4.47%, while those that increased on average increased by a whopping +11.75%.

71% of languages that showed a significant change in APPROXIMANTS decreased by an average of -5.73%.

FLAP-TAPS and TRILLS are harder to compare, as not all languages include either. However, of the languages that include them, no conclusions could be made, as increases and decreases happened relatively equally.

Similar to the flap-taps and trills, LATERAL APPROXIMATES displayed no bias towards a significant increase or decrease. Furthermore, the changes varied by relatively small amounts.

These observations were taken from all the languages, and surprisingly did not have strong correlation within a language family. Many languages in the same family tended to lose or gain similar amounts in each manner, but frequently, lost or gained huge in different directs on key manners. For example, the Uralic language family showed large (+10%) increases in stop syllables, but the individual languages showed switching fluctuations when it came to non-sibilant fricatives.

However, these trends could be identified from the individual families.


Latin - Inconsistent (especially French)

Germanic - Decrease in Non-Sibilant Fricatives (very surprising to us), decrease in Approximants

North Germanic - Increase in Non-Sibilant Fricatives, decrease in Approximants

Slavic - Decrease in Nasals, increase in Stops, decrease in Sibilant Fricatives

Uralic - Decrease in Nasals, increase in Stops, decrease in Approximants

Indic - Decrease in Nasals, increase in Sibilant Fricatives

Sino-Tibetan - Inconsistent

Austric - Increase in Sibilant Fricatives

The above families are listed in order of geographical and temporal closeness, and much of what is seen matches between close families, especially the Germanic to Northern Germanic, as well as the Slavic to Uralic families.

Overall, there exists a large discrepancy between the sound of vulgarities and normal languag, proving our initial hypothesis. We attribute mainly this to the effect of emotionality and context on speech, but more research should be done to further determine the exact causes. Research Team Bio

We are a team of college students seeking to understand the relationship between the meaning and sound of words. The project was first created as a final project in the honors class "Digital Humanities" at The University of Texas at Dallas.
